\chapter{Phonemes}

\section{Vowels}

\subsection{Monophthongs}

Interturkic has eight vowel phonemes, distinguishing them based on their
backness, height, and roundedness (table~\vref{tbl:vowel-inventory}).
Note how this forms a perfectly symmetrical inventory, with each vowel having a
counterpart along each of these axes;
\phonem{œ}'s back equivalent is \phonem{ɔ}, its high equivalent is \phonem{y},
and its unrounded equivalent is \phonem{ɛ}.

\begin{table}
  \centering
  \caption{Vowel inventory}
  \label{tbl:vowel-inventory}
  \begin{tabular}{lllll}
    \toprule
    &\multicolumn{2}{l}{Front}&\multicolumn{2}{l}{Back}\\
    \cmidrule(lr){2-3}\cmidrule(lr){4-5}
    &unrounded&rounded&unrounded&rounded\\
    \midrule
    High&\phonem{i}&\phonem{y}&\phonem{ɯ}&\phonem{u}\\
    Low&\phonem{ɛ}&\phonem{œ}&\phonem{ɑ}&\phonem{ɔ}\\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

In native items, \phonem{œ} and \phonem{ɔ} occur in only the first syllables of
roots.
All of the other vowels\---as well as \phonem{œ} and \phonem{ɔ} in loans\---are
unrestricted in their distribution, and may occur in any position.

\subsection{Diphthongs}

There exists a small set of diphthongs, though they are not phonemic in their
own right.
They are formed from\---and analysed as\---sequences of monophthongs followed by
\phonem{j} and \phonem{w}~(\vref{ex:diphthongs-yes}).
Note that such sequences are not considered diphthongs if the monophthong and
glide in question are split from each other across syllable
boundaries~(\vref{ex:diphthongs-no}).

\pex
  \a\label{ex:diphthongs-yes}%
    \vtop{\halign{
      \phonem{#}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil\cr
      ɑj&moon, month\cr
      tɑw&mountain\cr
    }}
  \a\label{ex:diphthongs-no}%
    \vtop{\halign{
      \phonem{#}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil\cr
      bɔ.jun&nose\cr
      ɑ.wul&village\cr
    }}
\xe

\section{Consonants}

Interturkic has 26 consonant phonemes, with their defining characteristics being
their voicing and manner of articulation (table~\vref{tbl:consonant-inventory}).
Of these, \phonem{f, h, v, ʒ, ʔ} occur only in loans.

\begin{table}
  \centering
  \caption{Consonant inventory}
  \label{tbl:consonant-inventory}
  \begin{tabular}{lll}
    \toprule
    &Unvoiced&Voiced\\
    \midrule
    Nasal&&\phonem{m n ŋ}\\
    Stop&\phonem{p t k q ʔ}&\phonem{b d g}\\
    Affricate&\phonem{t͡ʃ}&\phonem{d͡ʒ}\\
    Fricative&\phonem{f s ʃ x h}&\phonem{v z ʒ ɣ}\\
    Liquid&&\phonem{ɾ l}\\
    Glide&&\phonem{w j}\\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

In native items, \phonem{k} occurs in complementary distribution with \phonem{q}
and \phonem{g} with \phonem{ɣ}, where \phonem{k, g} appear only in front\-/%
vowel environments and \phonem{q, ɣ} only in back\-/vowel environments~(\vref
{ex:native-velars}).
In loans, \phonem{k, q, g, ɣ} are all fully phonemic, and the aforementioned
distribution might not always hold~(\vref{ex:loan-velars}).

\pex
  \a\label{ex:native-velars}%
    \vtop{\halign{
      \phonem{#}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil\cr
      kɛl\=/&come\cr
      qɑl\=/&stay, remain\cr
      bœlgɛn&divided\cr
      bɔlɣɑn&become\cr
    }}
  \a\label{ex:loan-velars}%
    \vtop{\halign{
      \phonem{#}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil&\:\copiedfrom~\foreign{#}\hfil&\:(#)%
      \hfil\cr
      kɑɣɑz&paper&kāġaz&Persian\cr
      mɛkɑn&place, status&makān&Arabic\cr
    }}
\xe
