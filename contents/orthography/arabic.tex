\chapter{Arabic}

\section{Main letters}

The Interturkic Arabic orthography has 34 main letters, as shown in table~\ref{%
tbl:arabic-alphabet}.
Owing to the script's cursive nature, a letter's shape changes based on its
position within a word.

Each letter has up to four positional forms, isolate (not joined on either
side), initial (joined to the following letter), medial (joined to both the
preceding and following letters), and final (joined to the preceding letter).
Letters that have only two positional forms have essentially fused their medial
forms into their final forms, and their initial forms into isolate forms.
Therefore, they do not connect to any following letter.

\foreign{\latntxt{Se}}, \foreign{\latntxt{ulu he}}, \foreign{\latntxt{zal}},
\foreign{\latntxt{sad}}, \foreign{\latntxt{zad}}, \foreign{\latntxt{ta}}, and
\foreign{\latntxt{za}} occur only in Perso\-/Arabic loans.
Word\-/initial \foreign{\latntxt{alıf}} and \foreign{\latntxt{ayn}} are silent.
When \foreign{\latntxt{kiçi he}} represents a vowel, it does not connect to the
following letter.

\section{Secondary letters}

Word\-/initial \phonem{ɑ} in non\-/Perso\-/Arabic items must be written with a
\arabtxt{ﻣﺪەلی الف} \foreign{\latntxt{meddeli alıf}}.
In Arabic loans, it may be used in non\-/initial position to represent \phonem{%
ʔɑ}.

\ex
  \vtop{\halign{
    \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil\cr
    آلتمیش&altmış&sixty\cr
    قرآن&Qur'an&(the) Qur'an\cr
  }}
\xe

In addition to \foreign{\latntxt{ayn}}, \arabtxt{ﻫﻤﺰە} \foreign{\latntxt{hemze}}
also represents \phonem{ʔ}.
Exclusively in word\-/final position of certain Arabic loans, it can be written
on its own~(\ref{ex:hemze-independent}).
It may also be written with \foreign{\latntxt{alıf}}, \foreign{\latntxt{waw}},
or \foreign{\latntxt{ye}}~(\ref{ex:hemze-bound}).
These graphemes are respectively named \arabtxt{همزەلی الف} \foreign{\latntxt{%
hemzeli alıf}}, \arabtxt{همزەلی واو} \foreign{\latntxt{hemzeli waw}}, and
\arabtxt{همزەلی یە} \foreign{\latntxt{hemzeli ye}}.

\pex
  \a\label{ex:hemze-independent}
    \vtop{\halign{
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil\cr
      ابتداء&ıbtıda'&beginning\cr
      جزء&cuz'&juz (one thirtieth of the Qur'an)\cr
    }}
  \a\label{ex:hemze-bound}
    \vtop{\halign{%
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil\cr
      مسألت&mas'alat&(a) problem\cr
      سؤال&su'al&question\cr
      فائدە&fa'ıda&gain, profit\cr
    }}
\xe

When suffixes are joined to words ending with an independent
\foreign{\latntxt{hemze}}, the \foreign{\latntxt{hemze}} must be turned into a
\foreign{\latntxt{hemzeli ye}}.

\foreign{\latntxt{Hemzeli alıf}} and \foreign{\latntxt{hemzeli waw}} are used
exclusively in Arabic loans.
\foreign{\latntxt{Hemzeli ye}} may be used in non\-/Arabic loans.
In fact, it is the correct way to represent \phonem{ʔ} in such items.

\ex
  \vtop{\halign{
    \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil\cr
    هاوایئی&Hawai'i&Hawaii (US state)\cr
    شیئان&Şi'an&Xi'an (city in China)\cr
  }}
\xe

\section{Ligatures}

When \foreign{\latntxt{lam}} is followed by \foreign{\latntxt{aıf}}, it must
be written with the ligature \foreign{\latntxt{lam\-/alıf}}~(\ref{ex:lam-alif}).
This is the only mandatory ligature in the Arabic orthography.

\ex\label{ex:lam-alif}
  \vtop{\halign{
    \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil\cr
    بولامن&bolaman&I become\cr
    هلال&hılal&crescent\cr
    آرسلان&arslan&lion\cr
  }}
\xe

When used to render the word for \transl{God} in monotheistic contexts, the
sequence \foreign{\latntxt{lam +~lam +~kiçi he}} must be written as \arabtxt{%
لله}~(\ref{ex:llah-with-alif}).
In compound terms involving the Arabic \arabtxt{لِـ} \foreign{li-} preposition,
no additional \foreign{\latntxt{lam}} is added~(\ref{ex:llah-without-alif}).

\pex
  \a\label{ex:llah-with-alif}
    \vtop{\halign{
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil\cr
      الله&Allah&God\cr
      بسم‌الله&bısmıllah&in the name of God\cr
      استغفرالله&astağfırullah&I seek forgiveness from God\cr
      ان‌شاءالله&ınşa'allah&if God wills it\cr
    }}
  \a\label{ex:llah-without-alif}
    \arabtxt{الحمد‌لله} \foreign{\latntxt{alhamdulıllah}} \transl{praise be to
    God}
\xe

\section{Representation of vowels}

The diacritics in table~\ref{tbl:vowel-diacritics} are used both on their own,
and in conjunction with \foreign{mater lectionis} letters---\foreign{\latntxt{%
alıf}}, \foreign{\latntxt{waw}}, \foreign{\latntxt{kiçi he}}, and \foreign{%
\latntxt{ye}}---to represent vowels.
These diacritics are usually omitted, but may be used when clarity is demanded.

An additional source of complexity in Interturkic's Arabic orthography is that
it has essentially three parallel sets of conventions for how vowels must be
represented:
one for non\-/Perso\-/Arabic roots, one for Perso\-/Arabic roots, and one for
suffixes.

\subsection{Non-Perso-Arabic roots}

\foreign{mater lectionis} letters in all positions (see table~\ref{%
tbl:vowel-repr-non-pa}).

\foreign{\latntxt{Zabar}} followed by \foreign{\latntxt{alıf}} represents
\phonem{ɑ}~(\ref{ex:non-pa-zabar-alif}).
\foreign{\latntxt{Zabar}} followed by \foreign{\latntxt{kiçi he}} (in

\phonem{ɛ}~(\ref{ex:non-pa-zabar-he-ye}).
\foreign{\latntxt{Zer}} followed by \foreign{\latntxt{ye}} represents \phonem{ɯ%
}~(\ref{ex:non-pa-zer-ye}).
\foreign{\latntxt{Waw}} on its own represents \phonem{œ, ɔ}~(\ref{ex:non-pa-waw%
}).
\foreign{\latntxt{Peş}} followed by \foreign{\latntxt{waw}} represents \phonem{%
u, y}~(\ref{ex:non-pa-pesh-waw}).

\latntxt{meddeli alıf}} and \foreign{\latntxt{alıf +~kiçi hi}}, respectively.

\ex
  \vtop{\halign{

    آلتی&altı&six\cr
    اَیکی&eki&two\cr
  }}
\xe

pairs, differing only in what letter they are preceded by in word\-/initial
position.
\foreign{\latntxt{Ayn}} induces front\-/vowel readings~(\ref{ex:non-pa-ayn}),
while \foreign{\latntxt{alıf}} induces back\-/vowel readings~(\ref{%
ex:non-pa-alif}).

    }}

      اوردیک&ördek&duck\cr
    }}
\xe

\subsection{Perso-Arabic roots}

In general, roots borrowed from Persian and Arabic retain their original
orthographical shape, even in cases where Interturkic's usual conventions would
require more explicit representations of vowels.
All of the conventions described in this section should be understood as being
in addition to those for non\-/Perso\-/Arabic roots.

\foreign{\latntxt{Zabar}} on its own represents \phonem{ɑ, ɛ}, optionally
followed by a vocalic \foreign{\latntxt{ki\-çi he}} in word\-/final position~(%
\ref{ex:pa-zabar}), corresponding to Persian/Arabic short \foreign{a}.
\foreign{\latntxt{Zabar}} followed by \foreign{\latntxt{a\-lıf}}---as in non\-/%
Perso\-/Arabic roots---invariably represents \phonem{ɑ}~(\ref{ex:pa-zabar-alif}%
), corresponding to Persian/Arabic \foreign{ā}.

\pex
  \a\label{ex:pa-zabar}
    \vtop{\halign{
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil&\:%
      \copiedfrom~#\hfil\cr
      جَنگ&ceng&war&Persian \foreign{jang}\cr
      سایَە&saya&shadow&Persian \foreign{sāya}\cr
      وَ&wa&and&Arabic \foreign{wa}\cr
    }}
  \a\label{ex:pa-zabar-alif}
    \vtop{\halign{
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil&\:%
      \copiedfrom~#\hfil\cr
      کتَاب&kitab&book&Arabic \foreign{kitāb}\cr
      شَاه&şah&king&Persian \foreign{šāh}\cr
    }}
\xe

\foreign{\latntxt{Zer}} on its own represents \phonem{ɯ, i}~(\ref{ex:pa-zer}),
corresponding to Persian/Arabic short \phonem{i}.
\foreign{\latntxt{Zer}} followed by \foreign{\latntxt{ye}} also represents
\phonem{ɯ, i}~(\ref{ex:pa-zer-ye}), corresponding to Persian/Arabic \foreign{ī}.

\pex
  \a\label{ex:pa-zer}
    \vtop{\halign{
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil&\:%
      \copiedfrom~#\hfil\cr
      هِدایت&hıdayat&guidance&Arabic \foreign{hidāya}\cr
      کِتاب&kitab&book&Arabic \foreign{kitāb}\cr
    }}
  \a\label{ex:pa-zer-ye}
    \vtop{\halign{
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil&\:%
      \copiedfrom~#\hfil\cr
      دِین&din&religion&Arabic \foreign{dīn}\cr
      خمِیر&xamır&dough&Arabic \foreign{xamīr}\cr
    }}
\xe

\foreign{Peş} on its own represents \phonem{u, y}~(\ref{ex:pa-pesh}),
corresponding to Persian/Arabic short \phonem{u}.
\foreign{\latntxt{Peş}} followed by \foreign{\latntxt{waw}} also represents
\phonem{u, y}~(\ref{ex:pa-pesh-waw}), corresponding to Persian/Arabic
\foreign{ū}.

\pex
  \a\label{ex:pa-pesh}
    \vtop{\halign{
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil&\:%
      \copiedfrom~#\hfil\cr
      مُلک&mülk&dominion&Arabic \foreign{mulk}\cr
      سُلطان&sultan&sultan&Arabic \foreign{sulṭān}\cr
    }}
  \a\label{ex:pa-pesh-waw}
    \vtop{\halign{
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil&\:%
      \copiedfrom~#\hfil\cr
      انگُور&engür&grape&Persian \foreign{angūr}\cr
      رُوح&ruh&spirit&Arabic \foreign{rūḥ}\cr
    }}
\xe

\foreign{\latntxt{Waw}} on its own represents \phonem{ɔ, œ}, corresponding to
Persian \foreign{ō}~(\ref{ex:pa-waw}).
\foreign{\latntxt{Ye}} on its own represents \phonem{ɛ, ɯ}, corresponding to
Persian \foreign{ē}~(\ref{ex:pa-ye}).

\pex
  \a\label{ex:pa-waw}
    \vtop{\halign{
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil&\:%
      \copiedfrom~\foreign{#}\hfil\cr
      دوست&dost&friend&dōst\cr
      گوشە&göşe&corner&gōša\cr
    }}
  \a\label{ex:pa-ye}
    \vtop{\halign{
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil&\:%
      \copiedfrom~\foreign{#}\hfil\cr
      میز&mez&table&mēz\cr
      تیغ&tığ&blade, sword&tēġ\cr
    }}
\xe

\subsection{Deletable vowels}

Vowels that may be dropped in the course of suffixation (see \ref{%
sec:last-vowel-deletion}) are invariably written with only diacritics, and never
with \foreign{mater lectionis} letters.

\ex
  \vtop{\halign{
    \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil&\:%
    \derivedto~\hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil%
    \cr
    بورُن&burun&nose&بورنُم&burnum&my nose\cr
    عقِل&aqıl&intellect&عقلِݣ&aqlıñ&your intellect\cr
  }}
\xe

\subsection{Suffixes}

Given their more restricted distribution within suffixes and the effect of
morphophonological processes, vowels in suffixes can afford more economic
representations (see~\ref{tbl:vowel-repr-suffixes}).

\begin{table}
  \centering
  \caption{Vowels in suffixes}
  \label{tbl:vowel-repr-suffixes}
  \begin{tabular}{lrr}
    \toprule
    \multirow[t]{2}{*}{IPA}&\multicolumn{2}{l}{Representation}\\
    \cmidrule(lr){2-3}
    &\multicolumn{1}{l}{final}&\multicolumn{1}{l}{non\-/final}\\
    \midrule
    \phonem{ɛ}&\arabtxt{ـَە}&\arabtxt{ـَ}\\
    \phonem{ɑ}&\arabtxt{ـَا}&\arabtxt{ـَ}\\
    \phonem{i, ɯ}&\arabtxt{ـِی}&\arabtxt{ـِ}\\
    \phonem{y, u}&\arabtxt{ـُو}&\arabtxt{ـُ}\\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\foreign{\latntxt{Zabar}} by itself represents \phonem{ɛ, ɑ} in non\-/morpheme%
\-/final position~(\ref{ex:suffix-zabar}).
In morpheme\-/final position, \foreign{\latntxt{za\-bar}} followed by a vocalic
\foreign{\latntxt{kiçi he}} represents \phonem{ɛ}~(\ref{ex:suffix-zabar-he}),
while \foreign{\latntxt{za\-bar}} followed by \foreign{\latntxt{alıf}}
represents \phonem{ɑ}~(\ref{ex:suffix-zabar-alif}).

\pex
  \a\label{ex:suffix-zabar}
    \vtop{\halign{
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{(#)}\hfil\cr
      ـتَن/ـدَن&\=/DAn&\Abl\cr
      ـلَر&\=/lAr&\Pl\cr
    }}
  \a\label{ex:suffix-zabar-he}
    \vtop{\halign{
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{(#)}\hfil\cr
      ـکَە/ـگَە&\=/ke/\=/ge&\Dat\cr
      ـسَە&\=/se&\Cond\cr
    }}
  \a\label{ex:suffix-zabar-alif}
    \vtop{\halign{
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{(#)}\hfil\cr
      ـقَا/ـغَا&\=/qa/\=/ğa&\Dat\cr
      ـسَا&\=/sa&\Cond\cr
    }}
\xe

\foreign{\latntxt{Zer}} represents \phonem{i, ɯ}.
It occurs on its own in non\-/morpheme\-/final position~(\ref{ex:suffix-zer}),
and is followed by \foreign{\latntxt{ye}} in morpheme\-/final position~(\ref{%
ex:suffix-zer-ye}).

\pex
  \a\label{ex:suffix-zer}
    \vtop{\halign{
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{(#)}\hfil\cr
      ـِم&\=/im/\=/ım&\Poss.\Fsg\cr
    }}
  \a\label{ex:suffix-zer-ye}
    \vtop{\halign{
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{(#)}\hfil\cr
      ـچِی&\=/çi/\=/çı&\Denom\cr
    }}
\xe

\foreign{\latntxt{Peş}} represents \phonem{y, u}.
It occurs on its own in non\-/morpheme\-/final position~(\ref{ex:suffix-pesh}),
and is followed by \foreign{\latntxt{waw}} in morpheme\-/final position~(\ref{%
ex:suffix-pesh-waw}).

\pex
  \a\label{ex:suffix-pesh}
    \vtop{\halign{
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{(#)}\hfil\cr
      ـُم&\=/üm/\=/um&\Poss.\Fsg\cr
    }}
  \a\label{ex:suffix-pesh-waw}
    \vtop{\halign{
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{(#)}\hfil\cr
      ـچُو&\=/çü/\=/çu&\Denom\cr
    }}
\xe

The convention on word\-/initial vowels is mostly the same as in non\-/Perso\-/%
Arabic roots, though \foreign{\latntxt{alıf}} might not necessarily imply a
front vowel and \foreign{\latntxt{ayn}} might not necessarily imply a back
vowel.

\section{Other diacritics}

\foreign{\latntxt{Sükün}} \arabtxt{سکون} is a small circle (\arabtxt{ـْ}).
Its primary function is to mark the absence of a vowel at that location~(\ref{%
ex:sukun-no-vowel}).
When combined with \foreign{\latntxt{waw}}, \foreign{\latntxt{kiçi he}}, or
\foreign{\latntxt{ye}}, it also necessarily implies they are to be read as
consonants~(\ref{ex:sukun-consonant}).

\pex
  \a\label{ex:sukun-no-vowel}
    \vtop{\halign{
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil\cr
      قالْقانْ&qalqan&shield\cr
      تورْتْ&tört&four\cr
    }}
  \a\label{ex:sukun-consonant}
    \vtop{\halign{
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil\cr
      آیْلق&aylıq&monthly, salary\cr
      تاوْ&taw&mountain\cr
    }}
\xe

\foreign{\latntxt{Teşdid}} \arabtxt{تشدید} is a small W\=/like shape (\arabtxt
{ـّ}).
It geminates consonants~(\ref{ex:teshdid-yes}), but must not be used across
morpheme boundaries~(\ref{ex:teshdid-no}).

\pex
  \a\label{ex:teshdid-yes}
    \vtop{\halign{
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil\cr
      ایلّی&elli&fifty\cr
      ملّت&millet&nation\cr
    }}
  \a\label{ex:teshdid-no}
    \vtop{\halign{
      \hfil\arabtxt{#}&\:\foreign{\latntxt{#}}\hfil&\:\transl{#}\hfil\cr
      کوممک&kömmek&to bury\cr
      یوللر&yollar&roads\cr
    }}
\xe
